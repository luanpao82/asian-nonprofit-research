English vs Local Language

Do researchers in each country publish on the same topics in English-language international journals as they do in their local-language domestic outlets? This page compares research themes between English-language publications (from NVSQ/Web of Science data) and local-language publications (from domestic academic databases) for each country.

Data sources: English-language titles are drawn from NVSQ (Nonprofit and Voluntary Sector Quarterly) Web of Science data, filtered by the first author's country. Local-language titles come from each country's domestic academic database (translated to English for comparability). Both sets undergo identical text preprocessing before comparison.

Overview

Similarity Summary

Two similarity measures for each country: Jaccard index (overlap of top-20 keywords) and LDA cosine similarity (thematic similarity via topic modeling). Higher values indicate more similar research agendas between English and local-language publications.

Methodology

How This Comparison Works

What is being compared?

For each country, we have two corpora of article titles:

  • English corpus: Titles from NVSQ/Web of Science where the first author's country matches. These represent research published in English-language international journals.
  • Local corpus: Titles from domestic academic databases, originally in the local language but translated to English. These represent research published in local-language journals.

Jaccard Index of Top-20 Words

The simplest comparison: after preprocessing, we take the 20 most frequent words from each corpus and compute their Jaccard similarity (|shared| / |union|). This measures whether the same concepts dominate both literatures, regardless of how those concepts are distributed.

LDA Cosine Similarity

A deeper comparison using topic modeling. Separate LDA models are fitted to each corpus using a shared vocabulary (built from both corpora combined). The resulting topic-term probability vectors are compared via cosine similarity, aggregated using symmetrized max-topic matching—the same procedure used in the cross-country comparison.

Interpreting the Results

  • High similarity (>0.55): English and local publications share substantial thematic overlap. Researchers publish on similar topics regardless of language venue.
  • Moderate similarity (0.35–0.55): Some shared themes but also distinct research agendas. Certain topics may be “exported” to international journals while others remain in local literature.
  • Low similarity (<0.35): Substantially different research agendas. English-language publications may reflect international academic trends rather than domestic research priorities.

Caveats

  • Sample size imbalance: NVSQ samples are much smaller than local corpora (e.g., Korea: 70 vs 5,105). LDA estimates from small samples are less stable.
  • NVSQ ≠ all English publications: NVSQ is one journal; English-language publications in other journals are not captured.
  • Translation effects: Local-language titles were machine-translated (DeepL or similar), which may introduce vocabulary differences unrelated to thematic content.
