English vs Local Language

Do researchers in each location publish on the same topics in English-language international journals as they do in their local-language domestic outlets? This page compares research themes between English-language publications (from a systematic review dataset) and local-language publications (from domestic academic databases) for each location.

Data Source

English-Language Article Data

The English-language corpus is drawn from the dataset compiled by Onishi, Liu, Moldavanova, and Yamauchi (2025) in their systematic literature review:

Onishi, T., Liu, H. K., Moldavanova, A. V., & Yamauchi, N. (2025). What do we know about Asian nonprofit research? A systematic review and bibliometric analysis of 60 years of progress and regional variations. Nonprofit and Voluntary Sector Quarterly. DOI: 10.1177/08997640251375973

Data Collection Process

Onishi et al. (2025) conducted a systematic literature review of 2,188 Asian nonprofit studies published in English peer-reviewed journals from 1958 to 2022. The data collection followed a four-phase process:

  1. Identification: 3,670 articles were identified from the Web of Science (WoS) database using two categories of search keywords—nonprofit-related terms (e.g., “nonprofit,” “NGO,” “philanthrop*,” “civil society organization,” “social enterprise”) and geographic boundary terms covering all Asian countries. Keywords were applied to article titles, abstracts, and author-provided keywords.
  2. Screening: 232 non-English or non-research articles were excluded, yielding 3,438 peer-reviewed journal articles addressing Asian NPO-related studies.
  3. Eligibility: 168 articles about non-Asian countries (e.g., Georgia, USA) were removed, yielding 3,270 articles. An additional 927 articles that only mentioned nonprofit keywords without substantial discussion were excluded through abstract and full-text review, leaving 2,343 NPO-related articles.
  4. Inclusion: 155 articles were omitted because full texts were not accessible. The final sample of 2,188 articles was retrieved for analysis, including 201 from nonprofit-focused journals and 297 comparative studies.

Why Web of Science?

WoS was selected as the primary database because the study aimed to assess scholarly research published in impactful English-language journals. WoS is regarded as an authoritative database for capturing peer-reviewed scholarly articles and is widely used in bibliometric analyses (Singh et al., 2021).

Location Classification

Locations and regions were defined using the United Nations and World Bank classifications. Articles were assigned to locations based on the geographic focus of the research (the “Location” field in the dataset). The distribution is heavily skewed: China leads with 572 articles, followed by India (306), Bangladesh (149), South Korea (70), Taiwan (68), Japan (54), Turkey (44), Nepal (35), and Vietnam (24).

Local-Language Data

Local-language articles were collected from each country's domestic academic databases (e.g., KCI and KISS for Korea, CiNii for Japan, CNKI for China). Article titles were translated to English (via DeepL or manual translation) to enable direct text comparison using the same preprocessing pipeline.

Overview

Similarity Summary

Two similarity measures for each location: Jaccard index (overlap of top-20 keywords) and LDA cosine similarity (thematic similarity via topic modeling). Higher values indicate more similar research agendas between English and local-language publications.

Methodology

How This Comparison Works

What is being compared?

For each location, we have two corpora of article titles:

  • English corpus: Titles from the Onishi et al. (2025) Web of Science systematic review, filtered by location. These represent research published in English-language international journals.
  • Local corpus: Titles from domestic academic databases, originally in the local language but translated to English. These represent research published in local-language journals.

Jaccard Index of Top-20 Words

The simplest comparison: after preprocessing, we take the 20 most frequent words from each corpus and compute their Jaccard similarity (|shared| / |union|). This measures whether the same concepts dominate both literatures, regardless of how those concepts are distributed.

LDA Cosine Similarity

A deeper comparison using topic modeling. Separate LDA models are fitted to each corpus using a shared vocabulary (built from both corpora combined). The resulting topic-term probability vectors are compared via cosine similarity, aggregated using symmetrized max-topic matching—the same procedure used in the cross-location comparison.

Interpreting the Results

  • High similarity (>0.55): English and local publications share substantial thematic overlap. Researchers publish on similar topics regardless of language venue.
  • Moderate similarity (0.35–0.55): Some shared themes but also distinct research agendas. Certain topics may be “exported” to international journals while others remain in local literature.
  • Low similarity (<0.35): Substantially different research agendas. English-language publications may reflect international academic trends rather than domestic research priorities.

Caveats

  • Sample size imbalance: NVSQ samples are much smaller than local corpora (e.g., Korea: 70 vs 5,105). LDA estimates from small samples are less stable.
  • Scope of English data: The English corpus comes from Onishi et al.’s (2025) systematic review of Web of Science, which captures peer-reviewed journal articles but may not include all English-language publications (e.g., books, reports, non-indexed journals).
  • Translation effects: Local-language titles were machine-translated (DeepL or similar), which may introduce vocabulary differences unrelated to thematic content.
