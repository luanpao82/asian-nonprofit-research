Methodology

How Similarity Is Computed

Step 1: LDA Topic Models

For each included country, a Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) model is fitted to the preprocessed article titles, producing k topics. Each topic is a probability distribution over the full vocabulary—a vector of β values indicating how strongly each word is associated with that topic. The number of topics k is selected per country via perplexity elbow analysis (see individual country pages for details).

Step 2: Unified Vocabulary Space

Each country's LDA model uses its own vocabulary (the set of words appearing in its titles). To enable cross-country comparison, all country vocabularies are merged into a single unified vocabulary (4,250 unique terms across 5 countries). Each country's topic-term vectors are then projected onto this shared space: words not present in a country's original vocabulary receive a probability of zero.

Step 3: Topic-Level Cosine Similarity

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors, ignoring their magnitude. For two topic-term probability vectors A and B:

cosine(A, B) = (A · B) / (|A| × |B|)

The result ranges from 0 to 1:

  • 1.0 = the two topics assign probability to exactly the same words in exactly the same proportions (identical thematic content)
  • 0.5 = moderate overlap in vocabulary and proportions
  • 0.0 = the two topics share no vocabulary at all (completely different themes)

Cosine similarity is computed between every topic in Country A and every topic in Country B, producing a matrix of pairwise topic similarities.

Step 4: Country-Level Aggregation

To reduce the topic-level similarity matrix to a single country-level score, we use symmetrized average of maximum per-topic cosine matches:

  1. A → B direction: For each topic in Country A, find the most similar topic in Country B (maximum cosine). Average these maxima across all of A's topics.
  2. B → A direction: Repeat in reverse: for each topic in B, find the best match in A. Average the maxima.
  3. Symmetrize: Take the mean of the two directional scores.

This approach accounts for the fact that countries may have different numbers of topics. It asks: “On average, how well can each country's topics be matched to the other country's topics?”

Interpreting the scores:

  • 0.55–0.60: High similarity. The two countries' research agendas share substantial thematic overlap (e.g., Korea ↔ Japan = 0.563).
  • 0.45–0.55: Moderate similarity. Countries share some research themes but also have distinct sub-fields (e.g., Korea ↔ Taiwan = 0.537).
  • 0.35–0.45: Low-moderate similarity. Countries have partially overlapping but largely distinct research agendas (e.g., Japan ↔ Turkey = 0.355).
  • Below 0.35: Low similarity. The countries' nonprofit research focuses on substantially different topics.

Jaccard Vocabulary Overlap (Second Heatmap)

As a complementary, model-free measure, we compute the Jaccard index between each country pair's top 20 most frequent words:

Jaccard(A, B) = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|

Where A and B are the sets of top-20 words for each country. This measures raw vocabulary overlap without the assumptions of topic modeling. A Jaccard score of 0.4 means that 40% of the combined unique words in both top-20 lists appear in both countries' lists.

Why Exclude Countries Below 1,000 Articles?

LDA is a statistical model that estimates latent topic distributions from observed word co-occurrence patterns. Its reliability depends on having sufficient data:

  • Topic instability: With fewer than ~500–1,000 documents, LDA topics vary substantially across random seeds and runs. The same corpus can produce different topic structures, making any single run's results unreliable for comparison.
  • Vocabulary sparsity: Small corpora have limited vocabularies. When projected onto a unified vocabulary of 4,250+ terms, most entries are zero, making cosine similarity less meaningful.
  • Perplexity diagnostics fail: In small corpora, perplexity scores across different k values tend to plateau, providing no reliable signal for selecting the number of topics (Chang et al., 2009).

Individual country analyses (frequency, bigram, co-occurrence) remain valid at any sample size because they are descriptive counts rather than statistical model estimates.
