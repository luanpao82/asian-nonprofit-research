About & Methodology

This database presents a comprehensive text analysis of research article titles across eight Asian countries: Bangladesh, China, Japan, Korea, Nepal, Taiwan, Turkey, and Vietnam. The analysis encompasses over 16,000 articles spanning from 1984 to 2026, representing the breadth of nonprofit and voluntary sector scholarship in each country.

Data Overview

The total number of articles in the final sample across all countries is 16,155. Each country's dataset was compiled from local academic databases and bibliographic sources. Where the original language was not English, article titles were translated into English to enable cross-country comparison.

Text Preprocessing

All article titles undergo a standardized preprocessing pipeline before analysis. This pipeline is designed to reduce noise and surface substantive vocabulary by removing words that are common to all research articles regardless of topic.

Preprocessing Steps

  1. Lowercase conversion — All text is converted to lowercase to ensure consistent matching (e.g., “Social” and “social” are treated as the same word).
  2. Non-alphabetic token removal — Numbers, punctuation, and special characters are removed, retaining only alphabetic words.
  3. Lemmatization — Words are reduced to their base form (e.g., “organizations” → “organization”, “volunteering” → “volunteer”). This ensures that inflected variants of the same concept are counted together.
  4. Synonym collapsing — Variant spellings and abbreviations are mapped to a single canonical form to prevent artificial fragmentation of key concepts (see table below).
  5. English stopword removal — Standard English stopwords (the, and, of, in, etc.) are removed using the tidytext stopword lexicon.
  6. Domain-generic term removal — Additional terms that are common across all academic research regardless of field are removed to ensure that the frequency analysis surfaces substantively meaningful vocabulary rather than generic research terminology.

Excluded Domain-Generic Terms

The following words are removed from all analyses because they appear frequently in academic titles regardless of research topic and therefore do not convey substantive thematic information:

Research process terms: analysis, approach, approaches, method, methods, model, models, perspective, review, reviews, study, studies, dissertation, report

Generic descriptors: article, articles, paper, papers, base, based, case, cases, data, evidence, result, results, effect, effects, focus, impact, use, used, using, status, current status, special feature, special issue

Country names and demonyms: korea, korean, japan, japanese, china, chinese, taiwan, taiwanese, vietnam, vietnamese, bangladesh, bangladeshi, nepal, nepalese, nepali, turkey, turkish

Country names and their adjectival forms are excluded because they would trivially dominate the frequency counts for their respective countries without providing thematic insight.

Analysis Methods

Unigram Frequency Analysis

Unigram frequency analysis counts the occurrence of individual words (after preprocessing) across all article titles within a country. The top 25 most frequent words are reported for each country.

What it reveals: The most common single words reflect the dominant themes and concerns in a country's nonprofit research literature. High-frequency terms identify the core conceptual vocabulary of the field. The concentration ratio (what proportion of total word occurrences the top 10 terms account for) indicates whether the field has a narrow focus around a few key concepts or a broadly distributed vocabulary across diverse topics.

Example interpretation: If “volunteer” is the top term in Japan with 2,399 occurrences, this suggests that volunteerism is central to Japanese nonprofit discourse. A high concentration ratio (e.g., top 10 terms = 19% of all words) indicates diverse research foci, while a very high ratio (e.g., 61%) suggests a field dominated by a handful of key concepts.

Bigram Frequency Analysis

Bigrams are consecutive two-word phrases extracted from titles after the same preprocessing pipeline. The top 30 most frequent bigrams are reported for each country.

What it reveals: Bigrams capture thematic collocations that single words miss. They reveal how individual concepts combine into established research constructs and recognizable sub-fields. For instance, while “civil” and “society” each appear frequently as unigrams, the bigram “civil society” demonstrates that these words are used together as a compound concept rather than independently.

How to interpret frequency gaps: A substantial frequency gap between the leading bigram and the third-ranked bigram indicates a clearly dominant phrasal theme in the country's literature. A moderate gap suggests several competing thematic phrases of roughly comparable prominence.

Co-occurrence Pair Analysis

Co-occurrence pairs identify words that appear together within the same title, regardless of whether they are adjacent. Unlike bigrams, which capture only consecutive words, co-occurrence analysis reveals conceptual associations across entire titles, exposing the broader intellectual structure of the field.

What it reveals: Co-occurrence data expose which concepts are frequently discussed together in the same research paper. Terms that appear in multiple high-frequency pairs emerge as hub concepts—words that serve as bridges connecting diverse thematic threads. A field organized around a small number of hub concepts indicates a tightly integrated research landscape, while distributed pairs suggest more fragmented sub-fields.

Example interpretation: If “ngo” appears as a hub concept in Bangladesh (appearing in pairs with “role,” “activity,” “development,” and “social”), this indicates that researchers investigate NGOs in conjunction with a variety of complementary concepts, reflecting a multi-dimensional approach to nonprofit scholarship centered on the NGO construct.

Network Visualization

Network graphs provide a visual representation of the relational structure of vocabulary. Two types of networks are constructed for each country:

  • Bigram networks: Nodes are words; edges connect words that appear consecutively. Tightly connected clusters reveal thematic groupings where terms are frequently used in sequence.
  • Co-occurrence networks: Nodes are words; edges connect words that appear together within the same title. The topology reveals which concepts occupy central (hub) positions and which form peripheral clusters.

Technical details:

  • Edge weight reflects co-occurrence frequency
  • An adaptive minimum-edge threshold ensures readability, targeting approximately 80 edges per graph
  • Node size reflects degree centrality (how connected a word is to other words)
  • Colors indicate community clusters detected by the Louvain algorithm, a modularity-based method that identifies groups of words that are more densely connected to each other than to the rest of the network
  • Layout uses the Fruchterman–Reingold force-directed algorithm, which positions closely connected nodes near each other

Distinct color-coded communities represent sub-fields or thematic clusters that are internally cohesive but more loosely connected to other parts of the network.

Topic Modeling (LDA)

Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is used to identify latent thematic structures within each country's title corpus (Blei et al., 2003). LDA is a generative probabilistic model that discovers groups of words (“topics”) that tend to co-occur across documents.

Model specification:

  • Number of topics: k = 5 per country (default), adjusted based on perplexity diagnostics and qualitative inspection
  • Estimation: Gibbs sampling with 2,000 iterations, 1,000 burn-in, thinning every 10 iterations, yielding 100 posterior samples
  • Minimum term frequency: Terms appearing fewer than 2 times in the document-term matrix are excluded
  • Exclusion criteria: Countries with fewer than 20 valid documents or insufficient unique terms are excluded from topic modeling

Topic selection: To determine the optimal number of topics, model fit is evaluated across candidate values (k = 2 through 8) using perplexity as a diagnostic criterion, where lower perplexity indicates better generalization of the model to the data (Wallach et al., 2009). When perplexity diagnostics are inconclusive (as often occurs with small corpora), the number of topics is determined through direct qualitative inspection of frequency analysis results. This hybrid approach—combining quantitative diagnostics where data permit and content-based judgment where they do not—is consistent with recommended practice for topic modeling in small or domain-specific corpora (Mimno et al., 2011).

Interpreting beta (β) values: In the topic modeling results, the beta value represents the per-topic word probability—the estimated probability that a given term is generated by a particular topic. Beta ranges from 0 to 1, where higher values indicate that the term is more strongly associated with and more characteristic of that topic. For example, if “social” carries a beta of 0.405 in Topic 2, this means approximately 40.5% of the word tokens assigned to Topic 2 are estimated to be the word “social,” making it the dominant defining term. Lower-beta terms contribute marginally to that topic's identity. Readers should focus on the highest-beta terms as the primary conceptual anchors of each topic.

Topic distinctiveness: The degree of overlap in top-ranked terms across topics indicates how sharply differentiated a country's research sub-fields are. Some overlap (e.g., 10–15% of top-5 terms recurring) is expected and interpretable rather than problematic: in a limited vocabulary, some high-frequency anchor terms will naturally distribute across multiple topics. The remaining terms should be largely distinct across topics to support meaningful thematic interpretation.

Cross-Country Similarity

Cross-country comparison is conducted through cosine similarity of LDA topic-term probability vectors. This method quantifies the thematic overlap between countries' nonprofit research landscapes.

Procedure:

  1. For each country, the LDA model produces a topic-term matrix (rows = topics, columns = terms, values = beta probabilities)
  2. Cosine similarity is computed between all pairs of topic vectors across countries
  3. Country-level similarity is calculated as the symmetrized average of maximum per-topic cosine matches: for each topic in Country A, the maximum cosine similarity to any topic in Country B is identified, and these maxima are averaged in both directions
  4. Only countries with at least 100 valid documents are included in this comparison to ensure stable topic estimates

The resulting similarity matrix is visualized as a heatmap, where higher values indicate greater thematic overlap between two countries' nonprofit research agendas.

Software

Original analyses were conducted in R (version 4.3.2). Key packages:

  • tidytext — Text tokenization, stopwords, tidy text mining
  • textstem — Word lemmatization
  • topicmodels — LDA topic modeling via Gibbs sampling
  • tm — Document-term matrix construction
  • tidygraph, ggraph, igraph — Network construction, layout, and visualization
  • widyr — Pairwise co-occurrence computation
  • lsa — Cosine similarity for cross-country comparison
  • slam — Sparse matrix operations for document-term matrices
  • ggplot2, ggrepel — Statistical graphics and label placement
  • wordcloud — Word cloud generation
